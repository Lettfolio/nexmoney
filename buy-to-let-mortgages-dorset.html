Buy-to-Let Mortgages in Dorset

From your first rental property to a portfolio — personal or limited company, HMO or holiday let, we know the lenders that say yes.

Buy-to-let lenders typically require a 25% deposit and expect the rent to cover 125–145% of the mortgage interest at a stressed rate. Within those rules there's huge variation — in rates, fees, rental calculations and appetite for property types — which is exactly where a whole-of-market broker earns their keep. NexMoney arranges buy-to-let mortgages across Dorset for first-time and portfolio landlords, in personal names and through limited companies.

How lenders decide what you can borrow

Unlike a residential mortgage, buy-to-let borrowing hinges on the rental cover ratio: the expected monthly rent measured against the mortgage interest at a "stress" rate above the actual pay rate. Basic-rate taxpayers usually need 125% cover; higher-rate taxpayers 145%. Where the rent falls short, some lenders allow top-slicing — using your personal income to bridge the gap. Knowing which lender's calculation flatters your numbers can be the difference between a deal working or dying.

Personal name or limited company?

Since mortgage interest relief was restricted for individual landlords, most new portfolio purchases happen through a special purpose vehicle (SPV) limited company, where interest remains fully deductible against corporation tax and rental-cover tests are usually the gentler 125% tier. The trade-offs: slightly higher rates, personal guarantees, and company running costs. We arrange both and work alongside your accountant — the right answer depends on your tax position and long-term plans.

HMOs, holiday lets and the Dorset market

Dorset's rental market has two profitable specialisms. HMOs — common around Bournemouth's university and hospital — command higher yields but need specialist lenders, licensing, and room-by-room valuations. Holiday lets across Poole, Purbeck, Weymouth and the Jurassic Coast are financed on projected seasonal income rather than a standard AST rent, again by a specialist lender panel. We place both regularly, including limited-company holiday lets, and we know which local licensing quirks lenders will ask about.

Refinancing and growing a portfolio

Buy-to-let remortgaging follows the same six-month-window logic as residential — and it's often the moment to release equity for the next purchase. For portfolio landlords (four or more mortgaged properties), lenders assess the whole portfolio's health, so presentation matters; we prepare the business case lenders want to see. If speed is critical — auctions, chain breaks, refurb-then-refinance — we also arrange bridging finance with a clear exit onto a term mortgage.

Buy-to-Let FAQs

Your questions, answered

What deposit do I need for a buy-to-let mortgage?

Usually at least 25%, though a few lenders accept 20% and the best rates sit at 40%+ deposits. Borrowing is also capped by the rental income calculation, not just your deposit.

How much can I borrow on a buy-to-let?

Lenders want the rent to cover 125–145% of the mortgage interest at a stressed rate. Some allow top-slicing with personal income where rent falls short — we know which ones.

Should I buy through a limited company?

An SPV keeps mortgage interest fully deductible and usually gets the gentler 125% rental test, but rates are a touch higher and companies cost money to run. It's a tax decision as much as a mortgage one — take tax advice alongside ours.

Can I get a mortgage for an HMO or holiday let?

Yes, via specialist lenders. HMOs are assessed on room-by-room income; holiday lets on projected seasonal income — a strong niche in Dorset's staycation market.

Is buy-to-let regulated by the FCA?

Most buy-to-let mortgages are not FCA-regulated — the main exception is consumer buy-to-let (e.g. letting a home you used to live in). Our care and process are the same either way.
